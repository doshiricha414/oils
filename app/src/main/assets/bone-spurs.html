Bone Spurs

Bone spurs, also know as osteophytes, are common in the foot although they can and do occur anywhere in the skeletal system.  A bone spur is formed as the body tries to repair itself by added more bone growth to a normal bone area, often at the edge of a bone structure.  The spur is typically smooth rather than a sharp “spur” and often goes undetected unless it rubs or presses on nearby ligaments, tendons, nerves, or other bones.  Common places for bone spurs in addition to the feet are the spine, shoulders, hands, hips, and knees.

The abnormal bone growth can result from a number of problems such as abnormal movement in a joint over time or unusual tension where tendons or ligaments attach to the bone.  Even “wear and tear” from everyday activities can form bone spurs.  Some common activities that may form bone spurs in the feet are running, dancing, stress from being overweight, and shoes that are too tight.  Bone spurs in the shoulder may also occur with repetitive stress and strain.  The normal process of aging can also cause bone spurs.
