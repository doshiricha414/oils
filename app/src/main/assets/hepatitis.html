Hepatitis

Hepatitis means inflammation of the liver. That inflammation can come from a number of different sources the most common being from ingested toxins such as alcohol and the excess, or even the normal, use of certain medications. Other sources are viral, bacterial and fungal infections as well as others that include autoimmune hepatitis.

The present use of the word hepatitis normally refers to infectious hepatitis and the most common infectious hepatitis is a viral infection. There are at least seven types of viral infections of the liver. The most common of these are the viral infections known as Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B and Hepatitis C. And finally the most common viral infection of the liver with a chronic affect is Hepatitis C that is estimated to be a chronic condition in 4.4 million Americans.

The symptoms of any of the hepatitis conditions seem to be similar so blood tests are necessary to specifically identify the source. Common symptoms include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, loss of appetite, weight loss, jaundice appearance and/or itchy skin. Hepatitis A is the most common of the infections but is a mild infection that may come and go without even being detected as the body’s immune system protects itself and it has no lasting or chronic effect. Hepatitis B and C are more invasive and both can develop into chronic hepatitis and affect a person for a lifetime. As mentioned Hepatitis C is the most common chronic condition affecting 3 or 4 times as many people as Hepatitis B.

These are all blood disorders and are not passed to others through casual contact such as hugging, sneezing, coughing or sharing eating facilities. It can be passed through practices that have blood to blood contact including drug culture activities, body piercing with non-sterilized instruments and other activities more common to underdeveloped nations.
