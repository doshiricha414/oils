Laryngitis

Laryngitis is inflammation of the voice box (larynx) located at the intersection of the throat (pharynx) and the trachea. A brief encounter with laryngitis or acute laryngitis is usually the result of a viral infection or straining of the vocal cords, for instance while cheering at an athletic event.  Bacterial infections, allergies or chemical irritants are other possible sources. Long term laryngitis or chronic laryngitis is most commonly associated with acid reflux but can be the result of more serious concerns such as polyps or nerve damage.

The symptoms of laryngitis are of course losing the voice. This may be a raspness or hoarseness or a complete inability to make audible sounds. This is because sound is made by the two vocal cords that are a combination of cartilage, muscle and mucous membranes vibrating as air passes between them. With infection or strain the swelling will not allow the normal free vibrations necessary for speech.
