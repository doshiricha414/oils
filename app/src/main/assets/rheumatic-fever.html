Rheumatic fever is now a relatively rare disorder in developed countries although in the US there have been more reported cases in the poorer neighborhoods. This health concern is initiated from a strep throat (bacterial infection of group A streptococcus). If untreated, in a few situations (less than 0.3%), the antibodies that are created by the body to attack the bacteria begin to attack the body’s own tissue. Therefore some characterize it as a type of autoimmune disorder. This then causes an inflammatory response that is responsible for many of the symptoms reported. The parts of the body most commonly affected are the joints but it can also attack the heart, rheumatic heart disease (RHD), causing scarring of the valves in the heart.

Rheumatic fever is more common in children and the symptoms begin to appear 2 – 4 weeks after the occurrence of the strep infection. Symptoms most noted are joint swelling and pain primarily in knees, elbows, ankles and wrists. Since, in about half the instances, the heart if affected there can be chest pain or general weakness. Others have noted fever, abdominal pain, nosebleeds or skin irritations. Also noted are some instances of Sydenham’s chorea, a nervous disorder characterized by involuntary jerky movements.
